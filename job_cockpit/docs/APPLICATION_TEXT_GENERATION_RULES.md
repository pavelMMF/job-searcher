# Application Text Generation Rules

These rules apply to every generated resume summary, resume bullet, cover letter, LinkedIn note, and application message.

## Why This Exists

Recruiters increasingly see AI-generated applications. The weak signals are usually not formal AI-detector scores, but human-visible patterns:

- generic template structure;
- over-polished but empty phrasing;
- repeated buzzwords;
- claims not backed by concrete examples;
- factual inconsistency with the resume, LinkedIn profile, or interview answers;
- placeholder/debug text accidentally left in the application.

Sources reviewed:

- Business Insider, 2026: mistakes when using AI for resumes, cover letters and networking.
- Business Insider, 2025: recruiters describing robotic, emotionally empty AI resumes.
- NY Post/HuffPost reporting, 2024: recruiter red flags in ChatGPT job applications.
- arXiv, 2025: cover letters become less informative when AI-generated texts are mass-produced; more editing correlates with better outcomes.

## Core Rule

Generated text must sound like a concise human analyst wrote it from real experience. AI may help compress and tailor, but the final text must be specific, grounded, and interview-defensible.

## Anti-AI-Detector Style Rules

Do not try to "trick" a detector. The goal is to avoid weak mass-generated writing patterns that recruiters and ATS reviewers notice.

Generated text should:

- vary sentence structure naturally, without sounding theatrical;
- use concrete nouns from the candidate's real experience (e.g. SQL, Python, Tableau, retention, cohorts, LTV, GGR, dashboards, churn, ML);
- prefer short factual bullets over polished abstractions;
- include only claims that can be explained in an interview;
- avoid repeating the same cover-letter template shape across jobs;
- avoid overusing the vacancy's exact keywords in every sentence;
- avoid generic enthusiasm and corporate filler.

## Forbidden Patterns

Avoid generic AI-like phrases unless they are backed by a concrete result:

- `proven track record`
- `results-oriented`
- `dynamic`
- `innovative`
- `cutting-edge`
- `tech-savvy`
- `excellent communicator`
- `team player`
- `actionable insights`
- `leveraging my expertise`
- `fast-paced environment`
- `I am excited to apply`
- `I am thrilled to apply`
- `It is not X, it is Y`

Never leave placeholders or meta text:

- `add numbers here`
- `insert metric`
- `as an AI`
- `generated`
- `tailored for`
- prompt fragments
- hidden scoring notes
- agent/debug notes

## Resume-Specific Rules

Resume text should:

- use direct, compact bullets;
- include real tools, metrics, products, stakeholders, or business domains;
- prefer concrete nouns over abstract claims;
- avoid stuffing every keyword from the vacancy;
- preserve all real experience roles from the master resume;
- match LinkedIn and interview-tellable facts.

Good pattern:

`Built Tableau dashboards for marketing, product and finance teams; tracked retention, LTV, GGR and bonus-program performance.`

Weak AI-like pattern:

`Delivered actionable insights through innovative data-driven solutions in a fast-paced cross-functional environment.`

## Cover-Letter Rules

Cover letters must be one paragraph and under 100 words.

They should include:

1. role/company;
2. one concrete reason connected to the company or vacancy;
3. one concrete fit statement from real experience;
4. no exaggerated enthusiasm.

Avoid opening every letter with `I am excited to apply`. Prefer direct phrasing:

`Hi [Company] team, I would like to work with you as [Role] because ...`

## Quality Checks

Before accepting generated application text, check:

- no forbidden phrases;
- no placeholders;
- no internal/debug text;
- no invented facts;
- at least one concrete skill/domain/result appears;
- cover letter is under 100 words;
- resume bullets can be defended in an interview.
